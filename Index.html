China's Child Mortality Rate

Uncovering the regional inequalities impacting child mortality in China to accelerate progress

What is child mortality?

Child mortality refers to the number of children that have passed away before they make it to their fifth birthday expressed per 1,000 live births.

Overview

Over the past few decades, China has made significant advances in their economy, education, and health (Guo & Yin, 2016); as a result, making notable progress in reducing the mortality rate of children under 5 since 1850.

Data sourced from (IHME, 2017) and (Statista, 2021)

In 1850 the child mortality rate was 417 deaths per 1000 births. This means that all children born in 1850, almost 42 percent did not make it to their fifth birthday.

Fast forwarding to 2017 China's child mortality rates have dropped to just 13 deaths per 1000 live births.

Data sourced from (IHME, 2017)

China is one among the few countries that has already successfully achieved child mortality health goals for both;

Millennium development goal 4 (MDG)
(to reduce under-five mortality by two thirds between 1990 and 2015)

Sustainable Development Goal 3.2 (SDG)
(to reduce under-five mortality to 25/1000 live births or less by 2030).

However...

Despite the marked improvements in children’s mortality rate, China faces a series of major challenges especially with regards to regional inequality.

Rural society in China comprises of almost half of their population, approximately 45%. Yet, in 2019 the child mortality rate in rural areas was more than TWO times that of urban areas.

Why is this happening?

The major reason for this difference can be revealed through the disparity between medical infrastructures within rural and urban China. In particular, rural patients face barriers to equal resources to healthcare access.

Data sourced from China statistics yearbook (2020)

There are almost less than half as many medical institution beds per 1,000 citizens in rural areas compared to urban areas in 2019.

In even more isolated areas where there are only small township health centres and no hospitals, there are only 1.48 beds available for every 1,000 citizens compared to 8.78 beds in urban China

The development rate of these township health centre beds has also seen the least growth over the years, with only a 24% increase from 2010

Data sourced from China statistics yearbook (2020)

Although there have been continuous improvements there are still stark regional differences between medical professionals.

In 2019 there were less than half as many health technical personnel, licensed physicians and registered nurses in rural areas compared to urban areas per 1,000 people.

This leads to understaffed and unequipped health clinics. Furthermore, many village clinics are limited in their ability to operate in this primary care role due to a lack of both training and compensation for physicians (Liang, Macinko, Yue & Meng, 2019).

This continues the cycle of physician distrust and force patients to go directly to Tier II and Tier III hospitals which are usually located further away and more costly.

Although telemedicine could support these doctors, many villages also lack the stable Internet connection and advanced technology to make this happen (Collective, 2018).

Data sourced from China statistics yearbook (2020)

How will this affect the future?

A prediction was made using China’s child mortality rate from 1990 – 2017. This prediction suggests that China will reach a 0 child mortality rate in 2040

Data sourced from (IHME, 2017)

Splitting up the regions we can see a drastic time difference as Urban China will reach a zero child mortality rate in 2030, which is 10 years before the whole nation.

Data sourced from China statistics yearbook (2020)

This suggests that Rural China accounts for an extra 10 years before the whole nation reaches 0. As shown in 2030, child mortality rates in Rural China will stand at five.

The Focus

Although there has been a significant decrease in child mortality rates in rural areas and significant improvements made on health institutions; Rural areas appear as a critical challenge and must remain at the forefront of China’s development.

Rural areas should receive the most attention in improving child survival through enhanced policies and programmes in the Sustainable Development Goals era (He et al., 2017) . Ultimately to eradicate the barries caused by regional inequalities and accelerate progress.

Reducing child mortality rates is not only a statistical target it means saving millions of lives and providing them a chance to survive.
